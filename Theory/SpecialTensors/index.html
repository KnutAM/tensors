Special tensors

Some tensors have special properties and occur in many applications, for example identity tensors, 111, I\boldsymbol{ I}I, and I\textbf{\textsf{ I}} I, as well as the rotation tensor Q\boldsymbol{ Q}Q. Below, these, and additional tensors with specific properties are introduced.

Identity tensor

The identity tensors, 111, I\boldsymbol{ I}I, and I\textbf{\textsf{ I}} I, have the property that when multiplied (using the appropriate contraction for that order) with another tensor, the result is that other tensor, e.g. a⋅I=a\boldsymbol{ a}\cdot\boldsymbol{ I}=\boldsymbol{ a}a⋅I=a. Symmetric tensors

The most prominent examples of symmetric 2nd order tensors in continuum mechanics are the Cauchy stress σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ and the small strain tensor ϵ\boldsymbol{ \epsilon}ϵ. From a computational point of view, it is advantageous to use that these are symmetric, because then we only need to work with 6 instead of 9 components in 3d.

Any 2nd order tensor, a\boldsymbol{ a}a, can be decomposed into its symmetric, d\boldsymbol{ d}d, and skew-symmetric, w\boldsymbol{ w}w parts

In continuum mechanics, an example of a fully symmetric 4th order tensor is the elasticity tensor, E\textbf{\textsf{ E}} E, giving the stresses from the strains via σ= E:ϵ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}=\textbf{\textsf{ E}}:\boldsymbol{ \epsilon}σ= E:ϵ for a linear elastic material. If we then also include that it is major symmetric, we only have 21 independent entries.

Deviatoric and spherical tensors
